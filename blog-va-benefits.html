VA Aid & Attendance: The Benefit 90% of Veteran Families Never Claim

I have sat across from families who have been struggling for months — sometimes years — to afford care for a veteran parent or spouse. They've been paying out of pocket, exhausting savings, making impossible choices. And when I ask if they've applied for Aid & Attendance, the answer is almost always the same: "What's that?"

Aid & Attendance is a VA pension benefit that provides tax-free monthly cash to wartime veterans — and their surviving spouses — who need help with daily activities due to physical or cognitive impairment. Dementia and Alzheimer's are among the most common qualifying conditions. And it can cover care anywhere the veteran lives: at home, in an assisted living facility, in a memory care community, or in a nursing home.

It does not need to be paid back. It is not a loan. It is a benefit these veterans earned by serving our country.

Who Qualifies?

To be eligible for Aid & Attendance, the veteran must meet all of the following:

  • Wartime service: The veteran must have served at least 90 days of active duty with at least one day during a wartime period (WWII, Korea, Vietnam, Gulf War era, etc.) and received an honorable discharge
  • Need for assistance: The veteran must need help with at least 2 Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) — bathing, dressing, eating, mobility, or toileting — OR need supervision due to cognitive impairment or safety risks
  • Income and net worth limits: As of 2024, net worth must be below approximately $155,356 (this includes assets but has specific exclusions for primary home and vehicle)
  • Medical documentation: A physician must certify the need for assistance with daily activities
Dementia often qualifies even without physical care needs:

Many mid-to-late stage dementia patients qualify for Aid & Attendance based on their need for supervision alone — even if they don't yet need physical help with bathing or dressing. The cognitive impairment requirement recognizes that a person who cannot be safely left alone has a genuine need for assistance.

Don't Forget Surviving Spouses

This is one of the most overlooked aspects of the program. The surviving spouse of a wartime veteran may qualify for Aid & Attendance even if the veteran never applied during their lifetime. The surviving spouse benefit is lower (up to $1,562/month in 2025), but it can still make a significant difference in affording care.

If your mother is a widow of a Vietnam-era or Korean War veteran, she may qualify — even if she never served herself and even if her late husband never touched VA benefits.

What Aid & Attendance Covers

The monthly benefit is unrestricted cash — it can be used for any care-related expense:

  • In-home caregiver or home health aide costs
  • Assisted living or memory care monthly fees
  • Nursing home costs (above what Medicaid covers)
  • Adult day programs
  • Medical equipment, supplies, medications
  • Any other unreimbursed medical or care expense

How to Apply — Step by Step

1

Gather the DD-214

This is the veteran's Certificate of Release or Discharge from Active Duty — the most important document in the application. If it's lost, you can request it from the National Archives at archives.gov/veterans.

2

Get a physician's statement

VA Form 21-2680 (Examination for Housebound Status or Permanent Need for Regular Aid & Attendance) must be completed by a doctor. It documents the veteran's functional limitations and need for care assistance.

3

Compile financial documentation

Gather bank statements, investment account statements, income sources (Social Security, pension, etc.), and documentation of all unreimbursed medical expenses — these reduce your countable income and can help you qualify.

4

Find a Veterans Service Officer (VSO)

VSOs are trained advocates who can help you file the application for free. Find one through your county's Veterans Services office, the American Legion, VFW, or DAV.

5

Submit and follow up

Processing times vary — often 3–6 months. Benefits are NOT retroactive to before the application date, so apply as early as possible. Every month you wait is money left behind.

⚠️ Start early — this benefit is not retroactive:

Aid & Attendance payments begin from the date the VA receives your application — not from the date your loved one's needs began. If you've been managing care for months and haven't applied yet, you've already left thousands of dollars on the table. Apply now, even if you're not sure you qualify.

Common Mistakes Families Make

  • Assuming only nursing home residents qualify. Aid & Attendance covers home care and assisted living too.
  • Not counting unreimbursed medical expenses. Out-of-pocket costs for medications, care, equipment, and health insurance premiums can dramatically reduce your countable income — sometimes enough to qualify a family that otherwise wouldn't.
  • Transferring assets before applying. Unlike Medicaid, Aid & Attendance previously had no look-back period — but rules changed in 2018 and there is now a 36-month look-back for asset transfers. Get advice before making any moves.
  • Thinking the VA will reach out. They won't. You have to know to ask, and then you have to apply.
  • Paying someone to file for you. VSOs file for free. Anyone charging a fee to file a VA pension claim is violating federal law — don't pay them.

Other VA Benefits Worth Knowing About

Aid & Attendance is the biggest one, but it's not the only benefit available to veterans with dementia:

  • VA Home Based Primary Care (HBPC): A team of medical professionals who come to the veteran's home — doctors, nurses, social workers, dietitians — for veterans who can't easily get to VA appointments
  • VA Community Living Centers: VA-operated nursing homes that provide specialized dementia care
  • VA Caregiver Support Program: Training, coaching, and respite for family caregivers of eligible veterans
  • State Veterans Homes: Skilled nursing care at reduced cost for veterans — each state has its own program
They served. They deserve every dollar:

Veterans with dementia and their families have often given everything to this country. Making sure they access every benefit they've earned isn't just practical — it's honoring the sacrifice they made. Don't leave this money unclaimed.
