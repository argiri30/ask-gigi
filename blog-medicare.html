Does Medicare Cover Assisted Living for Dementia? The Answer Most Families Don't Want to Hear.

By Gigi Bratakos, RBT  ·  20 Years Dementia Care  ·  RCFE Owner
← Back to all posts

I'll give it to you straight, because nobody else will: Medicare does not cover assisted living or memory care facility costs. Not monthly room and board. Not the ongoing residential care that costs $4,000–$8,000+ per month in most parts of the United States.

This surprises — and devastates — families every single day. They've been paying into Medicare their whole lives and assume it's going to be there when they need it most. But the way Medicare is structured, it was never designed to cover long-term custodial care. And dementia care is long-term, custodial care.

Here's exactly what's covered, what's not, and what your real options are.

The Simple Truth: Medicare vs. What Dementia Families Actually Need

Medicare is health insurance. It covers medical treatment — doctor visits, hospital stays, surgeries, medications, skilled therapy. What it does not cover is the cost of having someone live in a safe, supervised environment because they can no longer safely care for themselves. That's called custodial care, and it's the core of what dementia families need most.

The SNF 100-Day Rule — What Families Get Wrong

Medicare does cover a stay in a Skilled Nursing Facility — but the conditions are strict and the coverage limited. Here's what you need to know:

  • A qualifying hospital stay of at least 3 days is required first. Observation status does not count — this catches families off guard constantly.
  • Days 1–20: Medicare covers 100% of approved costs
  • Days 21–100: You pay a daily co-pay (in 2025, approximately $200/day). Medicare covers the rest.
  • After day 100: Medicare coverage ends completely
  • The stay must be for skilled care (nursing, physical therapy, occupational therapy) — not just supervision or personal care
⚠️ The observation status trap:

Many hospital stays are classified as "observation status" rather than a formal admission. If this happens, the 3-day qualifying requirement is NOT met — even if your loved one physically slept in a hospital bed for 3 nights. Always ask: "Is my family member admitted or under observation?" It's one of the most important questions you can ask in a hospital.

What About Medi-Cal (Medicaid)?

Medi-Cal — California's Medicaid program — is different from Medicare. It IS designed for long-term care, and it CAN cover nursing home costs for those who qualify financially. But there are important caveats:

  • Asset and income limits are strict. In California, a single person must generally have very limited assets to qualify.
  • There is a look-back period. Medi-Cal reviews asset transfers made in the past 30 months. Giving money or property to family to qualify can result in a penalty period where benefits are delayed.
  • Medi-Cal covers skilled nursing facilities — but coverage for RCFEs and assisted living is more limited and varies by program.
  • IHSS (In-Home Supportive Services) is a Medi-Cal benefit that can pay for personal care at home — this is one of the most underused programs in California.
Start Medi-Cal planning before a crisis — not during one:

The worst time to figure out Medi-Cal eligibility is when your family member is in the hospital and needs placement in 48 hours. Ideally, consult with an elder law attorney about Medi-Cal planning well before it's needed. Gigi can help you know the right questions to ask.

So How Do Families Actually Pay for Memory Care?

The honest answer: most families use a combination of sources, and many are caught off guard by the cost. Here's the realistic landscape:

Private Pay (Out of Pocket)

The most common funding source. Families use retirement savings, Social Security, pension income, proceeds from selling a home, or family contributions. The average memory care facility in California costs $4,500–$7,500/month.

Long-Term Care Insurance

If your family member purchased a long-term care policy years ago, it may cover assisted living or memory care. Review the policy carefully — benefit triggers, elimination periods, and daily benefit amounts vary widely.

Veterans Benefits (Aid & Attendance)

One of the most underused programs available. Wartime veterans and their surviving spouses may qualify for up to $2,881/month tax-free through the VA's Aid & Attendance benefit. Read the full post on VA benefits →

Life Insurance Conversion

Some life insurance policies can be converted to pay for long-term care while the person is still alive. This is often overlooked — it's worth reviewing any existing policies.

Medi-Cal (for those who qualify)

As described above — but requires planning, asset review, and often legal guidance.

The Bottom Line

The system is not set up to make this easy. Understanding what's covered — and what isn't — before you're in crisis mode can save your family tens of thousands of dollars and enormous emotional strain.
