From Gigi's Experience

The hardest conversation families face —
and how to have it with love.

When it's time to move your loved one from home to a care facility, most families freeze. They don't know what to say, when to push, or how to honor the person in front of them while still making the right call. Here's what 20 years taught me.

01

Know when it's time — and trust yourself.

If you're asking the question, you're probably already there. The signs are real: safety risks at home, caregiver exhaustion, rapid decline in function, wandering, falls, or the person with dementia becoming a danger to themselves. You are not abandoning them. You are choosing a higher level of care than you can provide alone — and that is an act of love.

02

What to say — and what not to.

Never lead with "you have to." Instead, lead with them. Try: "I want to make sure you're safe and that you have people around you who can take care of you the way you deserve." Avoid arguing about their memory or convincing them of their diagnosis — that battle cannot be won and it robs them of dignity. Speak to their feelings, not the facts.

03

Be assertive without being authoritative.

There's a difference between taking over and taking the wheel. You can be firm and loving at the same time. If they resist, don't back down — but don't escalate either. Say: "I hear you, and I understand you don't want this. And I also need you to be safe. We're going to do this together." Calm, steady, and clear. You are the anchor.

04

Preserve their dignity through the transition.

Let them make choices wherever possible — which chair comes with them, what photos go on the wall, what they want for their first dinner. Involve them in the tour if they're able. Frame the move around community and care, not loss. Their identity is still intact even when their memory isn't — treat them like the adult they have always been.

05

The first 30 days are the hardest — for everyone.

Guilt, grief, and second-guessing are normal. So is resistance from your loved one. Stay consistent with visits, but don't hover — allow the staff to build their own relationship and routine. Most families report that within 30 days, their loved one has adjusted far better than expected. Give it time before you judge the decision.

These are the principles — but your situation has its own history, dynamics, and emotional weight. This is exactly what I help families navigate, one conversation at a time.

What Nobody Tells You

The services, benefits, and decisions
most families find out about too late.

The system is complicated on purpose — or at least it feels that way. Here's what Gigi wishes every family knew from the start.

🕊️

Hospice — It's Not What Most People Think

Most families wait too long to call hospice — or never call at all — because they think it means giving up. It doesn't. Hospice is a Medicare-covered benefit focused on comfort, dignity, and quality of life when curative treatment is no longer the goal. For dementia patients, that eligibility can come much earlier than families realize.

Hospice brings a team to wherever your loved one lives — home, RCFE, or nursing facility. That team includes nurses, aides, social workers, chaplains, and bereavement support for your family. It is one of the most underused, most powerful tools available — and it costs families nothing when Medicare or Medi-Cal is in place.

When to ask about hospice eligibility: If your loved one has stopped progressing, lost significant weight, stopped walking, or is having repeated infections or hospitalizations — it's time to have that conversation with their doctor. You don't have to wait until the very end.
🏡

Home Health — A Bridge Most Families Miss

Home Health is different from hospice — it's skilled medical care (nursing, physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy) delivered at home when someone is homebound and recovering or managing a condition. Medicare covers it with a doctor's order, and many families don't know to ask.

For dementia patients still living at home, Home Health aides can provide personal care assistance — bathing, dressing, medication management — and buy families more time before a facility transition becomes necessary. It can also help after a hospitalization, which is one of the most vulnerable and confusing times for dementia patients.

Ask the doctor directly: "Does my family member qualify for Home Health services?" Many physicians won't bring it up unless asked. You have every right to request it.
📑

What Medicare & Medi-Cal Cover — and What They Don't

This is where families get blindsided. Medicare does not cover long-term residential care in an RCFE or assisted living facility. That cost — which can run $3,000–$7,000+ per month — typically comes out of pocket or through long-term care insurance.

What Medicare does cover: hospital stays, doctor visits, skilled nursing facility care (short-term, after a qualifying hospital stay), home health, and hospice. Medi-Cal (Medicaid in California) can cover long-term nursing home care for those who qualify financially, but the asset and income rules are strict and the application process is complex.

Key things to know:
  • Medicare covers up to 100 days in a skilled nursing facility after a 3-day hospital stay — but coverage decreases significantly after day 20
  • Medi-Cal planning should start before a crisis — there are look-back periods for asset transfers
  • A Geriatric Care Manager or Elder Law attorney can help you navigate this — and Gigi can help you know the right questions to ask them
🏥

Assisted Living vs. Skilled Nursing — Do You Ever Have To Move?

This is one of the questions Gigi gets most often. The honest answer: not always — but sometimes yes. A good RCFE that specializes in memory care can often support a resident through end of life, especially with hospice layered in. The key is whether the facility can manage the person's medical complexity as the disease progresses.

A skilled nursing facility (SNF) becomes necessary when someone needs 24-hour licensed nursing care — things like IV medications, wound care, ventilators, feeding tubes, or behaviors that require a higher level of medical intervention than an RCFE is licensed to provide. Dementia alone is rarely the reason for the move. It's usually a medical complication on top of dementia.

Before assuming a move is required: Get a second opinion. Some facilities will push a transfer prematurely. Ask specifically: "What would need to change for my loved one to be able to stay here?" A good facility will answer that honestly.

Staying Home Is a Valid Choice

Nobody has to go to a facility.
But here's what staying home really takes.

One of the most important things Gigi will tell you: a facility is not the only answer — and it's not always the right one. Many families successfully care for a loved one with dementia at home for years. But it requires honest planning, real support, and knowing what you're signing up for before the hard moments arrive.

🏠

It's OK to stay home — really.

Choosing to keep your loved one at home is not denial. It's not naive. For many families, it's the right call — and it can work beautifully with the right structure in place. Familiar surroundings, family presence, and personal routines can actually slow cognitive decline and reduce anxiety for dementia patients. Don't let anyone make you feel like home care is the lesser option.

👁️

Safety first — do a real home assessment.

The home you've lived in for decades may need to change. Walk through every room with fresh eyes and ask: where could they fall? What could they accidentally turn on, swallow, or wander out of? Key areas to address:

  • Remove throw rugs and improve lighting throughout
  • Lock up medications, cleaning products, and sharp objects
  • Install door alarms or locks to prevent wandering
  • Add grab bars in bathrooms, stairway handrails, and non-slip mats
  • Consider a medical alert device or GPS tracker for safety
  • Secure the stove — stoves are one of the most common hazards
🗓️

Routine is everything — build one and protect it.

People with dementia thrive on predictability. A consistent daily schedule — same wake time, meals, activities, and bedtime — reduces confusion, agitation, and sundowning. When routines break (holidays, travel, visitors), behavioral symptoms often spike. Build a simple written schedule and share it with everyone involved in care. The routine isn't just for them — it's for you too.

🤝

You cannot do this alone — and you shouldn't try.

This is the part most families learn too late. One person cannot be a 24/7 caregiver without serious consequences to their own health, relationships, and mental wellbeing. Build a care team — even a small one. Rotate responsibilities among family members, hire part-time in-home help, use Adult Day Programs to give yourself a break, and explore respite care options. Accepting help is not weakness. It's sustainability.

📉

Know the signs that home care is no longer safe.

Staying home is the right choice — until it isn't. Watch for these signals that the situation has changed:

  • Frequent falls or unexplained injuries
  • Wandering outside, especially at night
  • Caregiver health declining — physically or emotionally
  • Aggression or behaviors that put others at risk
  • Inability to manage medications, eating, or basic hygiene safely
  • Isolation — both theirs and yours

None of these mean you failed. They mean the level of care needed has grown beyond what one household can provide.

💬

Use the resources that exist — they're there for you.

Most families don't know what's available to them. Adult Day Health Programs, Respite Care, In-Home Supportive Services (IHSS in California), Caregiver Support Groups, the Alzheimer's Association helpline, and your local Area Agency on Aging are all resources that can extend your ability to care at home significantly. Gigi can help you figure out which ones apply to your situation and how to access them.

Whether you stay home or eventually make a transition, the most important thing is that the decision is informed, supported, and made with love — not out of guilt, exhaustion, or fear. That's what Gigi is here to help with.

For Veterans & Their Families

They served our country.
Now let's make sure they get every benefit they've earned.

Veterans with Alzheimer's and dementia are among the most underserved populations when it comes to knowing what they're entitled to. Most families have no idea these benefits exist — or how to access them. Gigi does, and she's here to help you navigate every step.

💰 Aid & Attendance — The Benefit Most Families Never Hear About

Aid & Attendance is an enhanced VA pension — tax-free, monthly cash — for wartime veterans and their surviving spouses who need help with daily activities due to physical or cognitive impairment. Dementia and Alzheimer's are among the most common qualifying conditions.

It covers care wherever the veteran lives — at home, in an RCFE, assisted living, or memory care. It does not need to be paid back.

Up to $2,431/mo Single veteran (2025)
Up to $2,881/mo Veteran with spouse (2025)
Up to $1,562/mo Surviving spouse (2025)
Who qualifies? Wartime veterans (or their surviving spouses) who need help with at least 2 activities of daily living — bathing, dressing, eating, mobility, toileting — OR who require supervision due to cognitive impairment. Many mid-to-late stage dementia patients qualify even if they don't need direct physical help with every task.

📋 What You Need to Apply

The application process can feel overwhelming, but it's manageable with the right guidance. Here's what families need to gather:

  • DD-214 — the veteran's discharge document (proof of wartime service)
  • Financial records — income, assets, and unreimbursed medical expenses
  • Medical documentation — a doctor's note confirming need for daily care assistance
  • VA Form 21-2680 — the Examination for Housebound Status or Permanent Need for Regular Aid & Attendance
Start early — don't wait for a crisis. The application process takes time. A Veterans Service Officer (VSO) can help you apply for free. Gigi can help you understand what you're applying for and what to expect.

🏥 VA Health Care Benefits for Veterans with Dementia

Veterans enrolled in VA Health Care have access to a wide range of dementia-related services that most families don't know to ask about:

  • Diagnosis & testing — cognitive screenings, neuropsychological testing, MRIs, lab work — all covered
  • Medications — most dementia-related medications covered under VA health care
  • Home Based Primary Care (HBPC) — comprehensive medical care brought directly to the home
  • Homemaker & Home Health Aide services — personal care assistance at home
  • Adult Day Health Care — daytime programs providing supervision, activities, and medical care
  • Caregiver Support Program — education, support groups, coaching, and respite for family caregivers
  • Community Living Centers (CLCs) — VA nursing homes with dementia-specific care
  • State Veterans Homes — skilled nursing care in a community setting

⚠️ Common Mistakes Families Make with VA Benefits

  • Waiting too long to apply. Aid & Attendance is not retroactive — every month you delay is money left on the table.
  • Assuming the VA will tell them. The system does not proactively reach out. You have to know to ask.
  • Thinking it only applies in a nursing home. It covers home care, RCFE, assisted living, and memory care too.
  • Not counting unreimbursed medical expenses. These can dramatically lower countable income and help families qualify who think they don't.
  • Forgetting about surviving spouses. A widow or widower of a wartime veteran may qualify even if the veteran never applied.
  • Going it alone. A Veterans Service Officer or accredited VA benefits counselor — like Gigi — can make the difference between approval and frustration.

Every veteran deserves dignity — and the benefits they earned.
