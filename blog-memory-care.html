When Is It Time to Move a Parent to Memory Care? An RCFE Owner's Honest Answer.

By Gigi Bratakos, RBT  ·  20 Years Dementia Care  ·  RCFE Owner
← Back to all posts

This is the question I get asked more than any other. And it's the one that carries the most weight — because getting it wrong in either direction has real consequences. Move too early, and families carry guilt for years. Wait too long, and everyone suffers — including the person with dementia.

After 20 years of owning and operating Residential Care Facilities for the Elderly (RCFEs), and after caring for my own grandmother with dementia in my home for 10 years, here's what I can tell you with confidence: there is no perfect moment. But there are clear signs.

First — Let Go of the Guilt

Most families wait too long because they feel that moving a parent to memory care means they've failed. It doesn't. It means the level of care needed has grown beyond what one household can safely provide — and choosing a higher level of care is one of the most loving things you can do.

I've sat with hundreds of families who made this decision. Not one of them stopped loving their parent. Every single one of them was exhausted, scared, and doing their absolute best.

The reframe that helps:

You are not placing your parent somewhere. You are choosing a community with trained staff, 24-hour support, and specialized programs — things no single family can replicate at home alone.

The Signs It's Time

These are the signals I look for — the ones families often explain away or minimize until they can't anymore:

Safety has become a serious concern

  • Frequent falls, unexplained bruises, or near-misses that are increasing
  • Wandering outside — especially at night or in bad weather
  • Leaving the stove on, flooding the bathroom, or getting lost in their own home
  • Inability to call for help if something goes wrong

You are burning out

  • You're not sleeping because you're on alert all night
  • Your own health — physical or mental — is declining
  • You've stopped seeing friends, working, or doing anything for yourself
  • You feel resentment, and then guilt about the resentment

Their needs have exceeded your capacity

  • They need help with bathing, dressing, eating, or toileting that you can't safely provide
  • Behavioral symptoms — aggression, paranoia, severe agitation — are beyond what home management can address
  • They've stopped recognizing you or other family members consistently
  • Medical complexity is increasing faster than your ability to manage it
⚠️ The sign most families miss:

When the person with dementia starts being left alone — even for short periods — because the caregiver simply has to sleep, shower, or work. This is the moment when home care has quietly become unsafe, even if nothing bad has happened yet.

What to Look for in a Memory Care Facility

Not all memory care is equal. As someone who has run these facilities, here's what I look for when I walk in the door:

  • Staff turnover rate — high turnover means residents never build relationships with caregivers, which is devastating for dementia patients
  • Staff-to-resident ratio — ask specifically. During the day AND at night.
  • How staff respond to difficult behavior — do they redirect, or do they medicate? Ask directly.
  • Whether the environment feels calm — not institutional. Not chaotic. Calm.
  • Activity programming — is there a genuine daily schedule, or are residents parked in front of a TV?
  • Whether residents look cared for — clean, dressed, engaged. Trust what you see.

How to Have the Conversation

If you're not sure how to actually tell your parent it's time — or they're resisting — that's a whole conversation of its own. The short version: lead with their feelings, not the facts. Don't argue about their memory. Don't try to convince them with logic. Say something like:

"I want to make sure you're safe and that you have people around you who can take care of you the way you deserve. We're going to do this together."

And then hold the line — lovingly and firmly.

The first 30 days:

Adjustment takes time. Most residents settle in within 30 days. Visit consistently, but don't hover — let the staff build their own relationship. Most families are genuinely surprised by how well their loved one adapts. Give it time before you second-guess the decision.

Every Situation Is Different

These are principles — not a checklist that applies to every family equally. Your history, your parent's personality, your home situation, and your support network all factor in. This is exactly what I help families think through, one conversation at a time.

If you're asking the question, you're probably closer to the answer than you think.
