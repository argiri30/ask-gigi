Sundowning: Why It Happens and What Actually Helps

By Gigi Bratakos, RBT  ·  20 Years Dementia Care  ·  RCFE Owner
← Back to all posts

Every evening, like clockwork, something shifts. The person who was calm and manageable during the day becomes agitated, confused, restless, or even aggressive as the sun goes down. They want to go home — even when they're already home. They think it's a different decade. They pace. They can't be redirected. They're distressed in a way that's hard to watch and hard to manage.

This is sundowning. And it is one of the most exhausting, disorienting experiences in dementia caregiving — for everyone involved.

After 20 years in this field, including caring for my own grandmother, here's what I know about why it happens and what genuinely helps.

What Is Sundowning?

Sundowning — formally called "late-day confusion" — is a cluster of symptoms that tend to worsen in the late afternoon and evening hours in people with Alzheimer's and dementia. It's not a separate disease. It's a predictable pattern of behavioral change tied to the time of day.

Symptoms vary widely but commonly include:

  • Increased agitation, anxiety, or irritability
  • Confusion about time, place, or identity
  • Pacing, restlessness, or inability to settle
  • "I want to go home" — even when they are home
  • Seeing or hearing things that aren't there (hallucinations)
  • Suspicion, paranoia, or accusations
  • Becoming demanding, clingy, or upset
  • Difficulty sleeping and nighttime wandering

Why Does It Happen?

Researchers don't have a complete explanation, but several factors contribute:

The internal clock is damaged

Dementia disrupts the brain's circadian rhythm — the internal 24-hour clock that regulates sleep, wakefulness, and hormone production. When this clock is damaged, the brain loses its ability to properly signal the transition from day to evening, creating confusion and agitation.

Fatigue accumulates throughout the day

People with dementia work incredibly hard all day to compensate for cognitive deficits — to follow conversations, navigate their environment, manage anxiety. By late afternoon, that effort has accumulated and their coping capacity is depleted. What felt manageable at 10am feels overwhelming at 5pm.

Light changes trigger confusion

As natural light fades and shadows increase, the visual environment becomes harder for a person with dementia to interpret. Shadows can look like people. Familiar rooms can look strange. This visual confusion amplifies the anxiety they're already feeling.

Hunger and physical discomfort

If dinner is late or they haven't eaten enough, blood sugar drops — and with it, mood and cognition. Unaddressed pain, a full bladder, or physical discomfort they can't articulate often show up as agitation in the evening.

The important thing to understand:

Sundowning is not a choice. It is not manipulation. It is not "acting out." It is a neurological event — the brain under duress doing what it can. Responding with frustration makes it worse. Responding with calm, structure, and redirection makes it better.

What Makes It Worse — Common Triggers

Known sundowning triggers to watch for:

  • Overstimulation during the day (too many visitors, activities, noise)
  • Under-stimulation or boredom (nothing to engage them during the day)
  • Disrupted sleep schedule or too much daytime napping
  • Hunger or thirst — even mild dehydration worsens confusion
  • Pain or physical discomfort (UTI is a major one — often missed)
  • Changes in environment, routine, or caregivers
  • Low light and shadows in the late afternoon
  • Caregiver stress — they pick up on your anxiety
  • Too much caffeine, especially in the afternoon
  • Medications that affect mood or cognition — review with their doctor

What Actually Helps

There is no single solution that works for everyone. But here are the strategies I've seen make the biggest difference:

🌞 Increase light exposure in the morning

Bright natural light in the morning helps reset the circadian rhythm. Open blinds, sit by a window, or take a short morning walk when weather allows.

🕐 Keep a strict daily routine

Consistent meal times, activity times, and bedtime reduce the brain's uncertainty about what comes next — which reduces anxiety throughout the day.

🍽️ Move the largest meal to midday

A lighter dinner and a more substantial lunch can stabilize blood sugar and energy levels through the vulnerable late afternoon window.

💡 Increase indoor lighting at dusk

Before natural light fades, turn on indoor lights — especially in the areas where they spend time. Eliminate shadows that can cause visual confusion.

🎵 Use calming music

Music from their era — played softly in the late afternoon — can be remarkably calming. Avoid TV news or anything loud or stimulating.

🚶 Build in afternoon activity

A gentle walk or mild activity in the early afternoon can reduce restlessness later. Avoid overstimulating activities close to the sundowning window.

✋ Stay calm — your energy matters

People with dementia are highly sensitive to emotional tone. If you're stressed or frustrated, they feel it and escalate. Slow down, soften your voice, breathe.

🧩 Redirect, don't confront

When they say "I want to go home," don't argue. Try: "Tell me about home — what do you miss about it?" Redirect to memory and feeling, not logic.

When to Talk to the Doctor

While sundowning is common, a sudden or significant worsening should prompt a medical evaluation. Specific things to check:

  • Urinary tract infection (UTI): In elderly people with dementia, a UTI can cause a sudden dramatic worsening of confusion and agitation. It's one of the first things to rule out.
  • Medication review: Some medications worsen sundowning. Ask the doctor to review everything they're taking.
  • Pain: Unmanaged pain they can't communicate is a common hidden driver of behavioral symptoms.
  • Sleep disorder: If nighttime restlessness is severe, a sleep study or medication evaluation may help.
💊 A note on medication:

Sleep aids and antipsychotics are sometimes used for severe sundowning — but they carry significant risks in elderly patients with dementia, including increased fall risk and even increased mortality. These should be a last resort, used at the lowest effective dose, and reviewed regularly. Non-medication approaches should always be tried first and documented.

For the Caregiver: You Need Support Too

Managing sundowning every single evening — often alone — is genuinely exhausting. It disrupts your own sleep, your own evenings, your own sense of normalcy. Please don't try to do this indefinitely without support:

  • Ask about Adult Day Programs so your family member is active and engaged during the day — which reduces sundowning in the evening
  • Consider evening respite help even one or two nights a week
  • Join a caregiver support group — others who've been through this are invaluable
  • Talk to someone — a counselor, a friend, or a consultant like Gigi — who can help you troubleshoot what's specific to your situation

Sundowning is hard. But it's manageable — with the right strategies, the right expectations, and the right support.
