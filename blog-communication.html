How to Talk to Someone with Dementia When They Don't Recognize You

By Gigi Bratakos, RBT  ·  Personal Caregiver  ·  20 Years Dementia Care
← Back to all posts

My grandmother looked at me one afternoon and asked who I was.

I had cared for her in my home for years. I had sat with her through the nights, held her hand through the hard days, rearranged my entire life around her care. And she looked at me with completely clear, kind eyes — and had no idea who I was.

If you've experienced this moment, you know there are no words for it. And if you haven't yet, you may be dreading it. Either way — here's what I've learned, both personally and professionally, about how to navigate one of the most heartbreaking parts of this disease.

First: Understand What's Happening in Their Brain

When someone with dementia doesn't recognize you, it's not because they don't love you. It's because the part of the brain responsible for accessing stored memories and faces has been damaged by the disease. The love is still there — the filing system that retrieves it is broken.

More importantly: their emotional memory often remains intact long after their factual memory is gone. They may not know your name, but they can still feel whether you're safe, kind, and familiar. That's what you're working with — and it's more than enough to create real connection.

"People with dementia may lose the facts of who you are long before they lose the feeling of you." — Gigi Bratakos

What NOT to Do

Most instincts in this moment are wrong — not because families are doing anything wrong, but because the natural human response makes things worse with dementia.

✓ Do This

  • Enter their world — go with where they are
  • Introduce yourself warmly and simply: "Hi, I'm [name]. I'm here to visit."
  • Focus on feelings and connection, not facts
  • Use a calm, warm tone and slow, clear speech
  • Make eye contact at their level — sit down if they're seated
  • Use touch gently — a hand on theirs if they're comfortable
  • Talk about things that bring them joy — family stories, music, the past
  • Smile and stay present, even in silence

✗ Don't Do This

  • "Don't you know who I am?" — this causes shame and panic
  • "You just saw me yesterday, Mom" — reality-checking doesn't help
  • Quizzing them or testing their memory
  • Showing hurt feelings in front of them
  • Talking about them to others as if they're not there
  • Rushing the interaction or showing impatience
  • Correcting them when they call you by another name
  • Insisting they remember — it creates distress without benefit

What to Actually Say

The goal is not to restore the memory. The goal is to create a moment of safety and warmth. Try these approaches:

Introduce yourself without pressure

Instead of testing them, simply offer the information: "Hi Mom, it's [your name], your daughter. I came to have lunch with you today." Say it warmly, not as a correction. And then move on — don't dwell on whether it registered.

Follow their lead

If they think you're someone else — a sibling, a friend from decades ago, their mother — don't correct them. Ask yourself: does correcting them serve any purpose other than making you feel better? In most cases, stepping into their reality and having a warm conversation as whoever they think you are is far more loving than insisting on the truth.

Talk about what they love

Long-term memories — especially from their 20s and 30s — are often better preserved than recent ones. Ask about where they grew up, what their parents were like, what music they loved. These conversations can be genuinely joyful even when they don't know who you are.

Sit in the feeling

Sometimes there's nothing to say. Sit close. Hold their hand if they're comfortable. Play music they love softly in the background. Connection doesn't require recognition.

The hardest truth — and the most freeing one:

You do not need them to know who you are for the visit to matter. Research consistently shows that positive emotional experiences leave traces in people with dementia even when the factual memory of the event is gone. They may not remember you came — but they will feel the warmth of having been cared for. That matters more than you know.

When It Happens for the First Time

If this hasn't happened yet and you're bracing for it — know that the first time is usually the hardest. After that, most families develop a rhythm. They find the approach that works for their loved one specifically. They learn to show up differently, to let go of the need for recognition, and to find the connection that still exists underneath the disease.

Give yourself permission to grieve this. It is a loss. You're allowed to cry in the car on the way home. And then go back — because they still need you, and you still matter to them, even when they can't tell you so.

Taking Care of Yourself Through This

Not being recognized by someone you love is a form of grief. And it tends to be recurring — each visit can bring a fresh wave of it. Please take care of yourself:

  • Talk to someone who understands — a support group, a therapist, a friend who's been through it
  • Don't let the visit be the only time you process your feelings
  • Set realistic expectations before each visit — aim for a moment of warmth, not a perfect interaction
  • Celebrate the good moments, however small — a smile, a squeeze of the hand, a song they hummed along to
